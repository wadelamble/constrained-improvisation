# Prose Style Notes

This note records the manuscript's current sentence-level prose preferences. It is not a general writing guide. It is a calibration note for future drafting and revision.

## Core Rhythm

- Lead with the claim. Do not warm up.
- Sentences should enter already in motion, not idle through setup.
- Prefer propulsion over supervision. The prose should sound like it knows where it is going.
- Compress judgment into the sentence itself instead of stepping outside the sentence to manage objections.
- Avoid "charitable to itself" phrasing that softens or delays the point.
- Every word should earn its place.

## What To Avoid

- Throat-clearing openings.
- Meta-phrases that explain what the sentence is about to do instead of doing it.
- Qualification-heavy first clauses.
- Supervisory wording that sounds like it is managing the reader from outside the argument.
- Slow on-ramps that make the prose feel like it has to get permission before saying anything.
- Repeated framing once the content is already clear.
- Extra connective tissue that restates structure instead of carrying thought.
- "Generated explainer" phrasing that smooths the surface while thinning the content.

## Desired Feel

- Immediate.
- Pressurized.
- Rhythmic.
- Directional.
- More like a top-flight rhythm section than an engine warming up.
- Economical, but not brevity-obsessed.
- Probing and creative where the thought demands it.
- Dense with insight rather than padded with setup.

## Practical Consequences

- If a paragraph has a strong claim, put that claim early.
- If a sentence can begin with the operative point, do that rather than staging context first.
- Keep transitions functional, not ceremonial.
- Use clarification where needed, but make it dense rather than padded.
- When revising model-generated prose, look first for warm-up language and cut it.
- Do not confuse directness with thinness.
- Do not cut a useful sentence merely because it is exploratory, surprising, or conceptually ambitious.
- Favor density of thought over mere shortness.
- A passage may be longer if each sentence earns its place by carrying real insight, pressure, or conceptual motion.
- Once the content is in place, remove every unnecessary word.
- Prefer dry sentence surfaces with live conceptual content.
- Do not mistake rhetorical smoothness for good prose.

## Example Diagnosis

- Good prose enters on the beat.
- Weak prose arrives after a preamble.

Future drafting should bias hard toward the first.
