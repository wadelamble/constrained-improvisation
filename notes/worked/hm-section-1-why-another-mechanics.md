## Why Another Mechanics?

Lagrangian Mechanics already gives us a remarkably deep way to do physics. We write down an invariant quantity, extremize it, and recover the physical path. The particle, we may say, has "learned" what the right trajectory is by solving an optimization problem. While this global perspective perhaps feels less orthodox in physics than the local differential-equation perspective, only it suggests an answer to the question of why dynamical laws exist. Here we will discuss Hamiltonian Mechanics, the modern local formulation of mechanics, because it brings to light physical structure that is not as readily revealed in the Lagrangian formulation and makes generator algebra explicit in a way later physics will need.

Lagrangian Mechanics is naturally organized around whole paths. It asks which history is selected from among nearby possible histories. That is why it feels so well matched to the geometric picture already developed, where physics grows out of extremization and geodesics. Hamiltonian Mechanics organizes the same physics differently. It asks what a complete state at one instant must contain, and what hidden geometry that state carries once it is written in the right variables.

That phrase, hidden geometry, should not be taken as a warning that something obscure is coming. On the contrary, the claim is that something simple has been in front of us all along. A physical system at an instant is not just a list of positions and rates. It has internal structure. Some quantities come naturally in pairs. Some changes are not independent of one another. Some flows preserve more than the eye first notices. Hamiltonian Mechanics is the formulation that makes those facts visible.

One way to say this is that Lagrangian Mechanics tells us how nature chooses a path, while Hamiltonian Mechanics tells us how a state flows. The first picture is organized by extremization. The second is organized by conserved geometric structure. These are not rival stories. They are two views of the same physics, but they illuminate different things. Lagrangian Mechanics makes the selection principle transparent. Hamiltonian Mechanics makes the internal geometry of dynamical state transparent.

This matters because later physics will need exactly that hidden structure. In the world of rigid bodies and familiar trajectories, one can often proceed without dwelling on it. But as the manuscript moves toward quantum mechanics and then quantum field theory, structure preservation stops being a technical nicety and becomes part of what it means for a state to remain itself through time. Hamiltonian Mechanics is where that deeper structure first comes clearly into view.

So the task of this section is not to replace the Lagrangian picture, but to expose what it has been hiding in plain sight. We will begin not with the Hamiltonian itself, but with the move that reorganizes the state so its hidden geometry can be seen.
