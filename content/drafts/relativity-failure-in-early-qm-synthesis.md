## Relativity Failure in Early Quantum Mechanics

This first failure is the deepest one because it is not just a missing feature. It is a mismatch in the shape of the theory. Relativity says that influence must be local. An event can affect only what lies within its future light cone, and whatever later produces an effect elsewhere must somehow be carried through the intervening region. But early quantum mechanics is not built out of local carriers of influence in spacetime. Its basic object is the wave function, and the wave function is not, in the classical sense, a field spread through spacetime that passes influence from neighbor to neighbor. For one particle it is a complex amplitude over position space. For many particles it is worse, from the standpoint of relativity: it is an amplitude over configuration space, a space whose points correspond not to places in the world but to whole possible arrangements of the system at once. That is already a sign that the theory is not natively shaped for a relativistic world.

We can see the mismatch directly in the Schrödinger equation,

$$
i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t}
=
\left(
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V
\right)\psi.
$$

The important fact is not just that this equation looks different from later relativistic equations. It is that it is first order in time and second order in space. That asymmetry places it in the same broad family as diffusion equations rather than genuine relativistic wave equations. We do not need to belabor the PDE classification here because we will return to it in the spring-lattice discussion. For now the point is simpler. A relativistic propagation law should build in a causal cone. The Schrödinger equation does not. If one begins with a wave packet concentrated in one region, the evolved state immediately develops nonzero tails arbitrarily far away. The theory's state updates as a whole. It is, in that sense, an everything-everywhere-all-at-once object.

The same problem shows up in the free-particle dispersion relation. For a free Schrödinger wave,

$$
\omega = \frac{\hbar k^2}{2m},
$$

so the group velocity is

$$
v_g = \frac{\partial \omega}{\partial k} = \frac{\hbar k}{m}.
$$

There is no invariant speed built into this expression. Nothing in it knows about light cones. As \(k\) increases, the group velocity simply increases with it. This is exactly what one should expect from a theory built for Galilean kinematics rather than Minkowski spacetime. One must be a little careful in how one says this. The claim is not that ordinary quantum mechanics, used in its proper low-velocity domain, immediately lets us send intelligible messages faster than light. The claim is structural. The theory's native equation is not the sort of local propagation law a relativistic world wants.

This is also why early quantum mechanics tends, ontologically, back toward action at a distance. Consider the hydrogen atom. The electron is described quantum mechanically, but it is placed in a Coulomb potential that is simply already there:

$$
V(r) = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}.
$$

That is an extremely successful model, but what is it actually saying? It is not showing us a local disturbance propagating outward from the proton through some field and reaching the electron in a light-cone-respecting way. It is giving the electron a potential defined over space in advance and telling us to solve the resulting eigenvalue problem. As effective physics this is excellent. As a fundamental relativistic ontology it is thin. The formalism contains no dynamical local medium in spacetime that carries the influence. The effect of one charge on another is encoded globally in the Hamiltonian rather than locally in a mediating field.

So the real complaint is not merely that early quantum mechanics "gets relativity wrong" in some crude or sensational sense. It is that it is not built to express relativity's deepest demand, namely that causal influence be local. Its fundamental state is global. Its time-evolution equation has the wrong structural type for light-cone propagation. Its free-particle kinematics contain no invariant limiting speed. Its interaction picture relies on prescribed potentials rather than visible local mediators. All of this tells us the same thing. If quantum theory is to live comfortably inside a relativistic spacetime, then something more local than the early wave function is needed. We need an object attached to spacetime itself, one whose disturbances propagate with the right causal structure from the start. That object is a field.
