Hamiltonian Mechanics may need one more payoff note beyond geometry and flow. It is the place where generators become explicit as usable local algebraic objects. Lagrangian Mechanics already gives extremized histories and already supports symmetry arguments, but it does not foreground generator algebra in the same way. Hamiltonian Mechanics does. Once dynamics is written in phase space, continuous symmetries act through generators on functions of the state, and those generators close into an algebra through the Poisson bracket.

That matters later because quantization is not only a matter of replacing one set of equations with another. It is also a shift into an explicitly algebraic viewpoint. Ladder operators are one later expression of this. They are powerful precisely because they belong to an operator algebra that is not tied to one particular representation. In that sense, Hamiltonian Mechanics is an important precursor to quantum mechanics not only because it reveals symplectic structure, but because it makes generator structure explicit in a form that later survives quantization.

This should be used carefully. The manuscript should probably not claim that Lagrangian Mechanics has no generators at all. That would be too strong. The better claim is that Hamiltonian Mechanics is where generator structure becomes explicit, local, and algebraically central in a way the later quantum story will need.
