# Hamiltonian Mechanics

## Why Another Mechanics?

Lagrangian Mechanics already gives us a remarkably deep way to do physics. We write down an invariant quantity, extremize it, and recover the physical path. The particle, we may say, has "learned" what the right trajectory is by solving an optimization problem. While this global perspective perhaps feels less orthodox in physics than the local differential-equation perspective, only it suggests an answer to the question of why dynamical laws exist. Here we will discuss Hamiltonian Mechanics, the modern local formulation of mechanics, for two reasons. First, it brings to light physical structure, with an accompanying geometric and algebraic structure, that is essential to understanding the evolution of statistical states. Second, and relatedly, it expresses kinematic structure in the language of generator algebra, which is indispensable when formulating quantum mechanics.

## What Is There Besides Paths?

The first thing Hamiltonian Mechanics makes us notice is that a physical law acts not only on one exact trajectory, but on a whole family of nearby possible states. Suppose we do not know the exact microstate of a system, but only that it lies somewhere in a small connected region of possibility. We then let every one of those nearby possibilities evolve under the same exact law. What happens?

This need not mean a formal statistical ensemble sampled from a distribution. It can be something as ordinary as three balls tossed into the air, each with initial position and momentum known only to finite precision. One exact set of initial conditions would give one exact trajectory. But finite precision means that what we really have is a small cloud of nearby possibilities. It is that cloud, not the idealized point at its center, that Liouville's theorem is about.

The answer is not that the region stays rigid. It may stretch, shear, bend, and become surprisingly intricate. But under exact conservative dynamics it does not simply compress away. Distinctions among nearby possibilities are not crushed out of existence. The law stirs possibilities, but does not squeeze them into less possibility. This is the phenomenological core of Liouville's theorem.

That claim is easier to feel in a fluid picture. Imagine not dye moving through water, but a connected patch of the fluid itself. As the fluid flows, that patch may be drawn out into long tendrils, folded over, and deformed almost beyond recognition. But if the flow is incompressible, the patch does not tear, does not split, and does not lose its area. It remains one connected material patch whose shape changes while its amount does not. Liouville says that exact conservative dynamics treats a connected region of possibilities in the same way.

This is already enough to tell us that there is hidden structure here. If the law merely selected a path, there would be no reason for a small cloud of nearby states to behave with this very specific kind of incompressible fidelity. The fact that it does suggests that the space of states carries a geometry, and that the dynamics respects that geometry.

One can see the beginning of that geometry in the mathematics of ordinary incompressible flow. In two dimensions, if the velocity field is

``` math
\frac{dx}{dt} = u(x,y),\qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = v(x,y),
```

then incompressibility means

``` math
\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = 0.
```

This condition says that a small material patch does not locally compress or expand. In two dimensions it has a powerful consequence. There exists a scalar function `\psi(x,y)` such that

``` math
u = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y},
\qquad
v = -\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}.
```

So incompressibility forces the motion into a crossed form. Change in one direction is generated by variation in the other. This is not yet Hamiltonian Mechanics, because we have not yet said what the relevant variables are. But it is the pattern Hamiltonian Mechanics will formalize. The geometry of incompressible flow already hints that the state of a system may come with paired directions, and that the law of motion may preserve not lengths, but oriented area.
