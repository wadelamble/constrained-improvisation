## The Symplectic Geometry of Phase Space and Hamiltonian Dynamics: Claims

1. `[claim]` The real question is not what phase space is by definition, but why a Lagrangian theory on path space leads to instantaneous states organized into position-momentum pairs.
2. `[claim]` The same question includes why that state space should carry a local area measure whose preservation later appears as Liouville's theorem.
3. `[claim]` The action is a functional of an entire history, not initially of a state at one time.
4. `[claim]` Varying the action separates into a bulk term and a boundary term.
5. `[claim]` The bulk term determines which paths satisfy the Euler-Lagrange equations.
6. `[claim]` The boundary term determines how the action changes when an endpoint state is infinitesimally changed.
7. `[bridge]` The boundary term is therefore the first place where a path-based theory factors out the structure of a state at one time.
8. `[claim]` Once the question becomes "what data at one time count as a state?", the boundary term becomes decisive.
9. `[claim]` The boundary term singles out \(p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i\).
10. `[claim]` The action-response to an infinitesimal endpoint displacement has the form \(p_i\,\delta q^i\).
11. `[claim]` Canonical or conjugate momentum is selected by the variational structure itself, not by Newtonian habit or later Hamiltonian hindsight.
12. `[claim]` Velocity answers the question "how does configuration change along a path?"
13. `[claim]` The boundary variation answers a different question: "what quantity pairs with a small change in state at a moment?"
14. `[claim]` In general that quantity is \(p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i\), not \(\dot q^i\).
15. `[warning]` Momentum may be proportional to velocity in simple systems, but that is a contingent simplification rather than the general structural fact.
16. `[claim]` The exact functional form of the momentum is determined by the Lagrangian.
17. `[bridge]` In free systems, the kinetic part of the Lagrangian is inherited from spacetime kinematics, so the form of momentum is not ad hoc even though its role is first exposed by the boundary term.
18. `[claim]` The pairing \(p_i\,\delta q^i\) is a one-form statement: it acts on one infinitesimal variation of one state and returns a number.
19. `[claim]` The one-form measures first-order action-response to one infinitesimal state change.
20. `[claim]` Liouville is not about one infinitesimal variation of one state, but about a cloud of nearby states.
21. `[claim]` One infinitesimal direction gives only a line of nearby possibilities.
22. `[bridge]` The first local object that can measure a patch of nearby states is therefore a two-form.
23. `[claim]` Two independent infinitesimal variations span a little parallelogram of neighboring states.
24. `[claim]` The first object that measures the extent of such a patch is \(dq^i \wedge dp_i\).
25. `[claim]` The one-form is action-flavored; the two-form is ensemble-flavored.
26. `[claim]` A one-form gives line data, while a two-form gives area data.
27. `[claim]` A line-supported ensemble in a two-dimensional phase space is singular: it has zero area and cannot support an ordinary phase-space density.
28. `[claim]` A generic ensemble in a two-dimensional phase space must therefore be represented by area elements, not line elements.
29. `[claim]` The one-form is not fully intrinsic, because adding a total time derivative to the Lagrangian can change it without changing the equations of motion.
30. `[claim]` The two-form built from the one-form is insensitive to that ambiguity and is therefore more intrinsic.
31. `[bridge]` The logic chain is: action \(\rightarrow\) boundary pairing \(\rightarrow\) one-form \(\rightarrow\) intrinsic two-form \(\rightarrow\) local state-space patches.
32. `[claim]` Phase space is the space whose points are instantaneous states expressed in the conjugate variables selected by the action.
33. `[warning]` Phase space is not, in general, position-velocity space.
34. `[claim]` For one degree of freedom, a phase-space point is \((q,p)\); for many degrees of freedom it is \((q^1,\dots,q^n,p_1,\dots,p_n)\).
35. `[claim]` Once \(p_i = \partial L/\partial \dot q^i\) has been selected, the next problem is to rewrite the theory in terms of \((q,p)\) rather than \((q,\dot q)\).
36. `[claim]` The Legendre transform is the device that performs that rewrite.
37. `[claim]` A Legendre transform does not need to transform all the variables of a function; it may transform only a chosen subset and carry the others along as parameters.
38. `[claim]` In mechanics, the transform is taken with respect to the velocity variables \(\dot q^i\), not the position variables \(q^i\).
39. `[claim]` The Legendre transform does not create the pairing between \(q\) and \(p\); it follows a pairing already exposed by the action.
40. `[claim]` Its mechanical significance is the cancellation of the unwanted \(d\dot q^i\) terms in \(dH\).
41. `[claim]` That cancellation is the mechanism by which the theory is rewritten from \((q,\dot q)\) language into \((q,p)\) language.
42. `[claim]` The first stage of the Legendre rewrite uses only the definition of momentum and the transform, not the equations of motion.
43. `[claim]` Bringing in the Euler-Lagrange equations turns the differential of \(H\) into the cross-coupled form from which Hamilton's equations can be read off.
44. `[claim]` Under suitable conditions, no physics is lost in passing from \(L\) to \(H\); they are equivalent rewritings of the same dynamics.
45. `[claim]` The point of the Hamiltonian rewrite is that, once \(p\) has been identified as conjugate to \(q\), the dynamics can be expressed as first-order time evolution on the space of instantaneous states.
46. `[picture]` A point of phase space is one exact instantaneous state.
47. `[picture]` As time evolves, such a point traces out a curve in phase space.
48. `[picture]` A family of nearby points traces a moving patch.
49. `[picture]` The moving patch is the first visible object on which the Liouville story can later be seen.
50. `[bridge]` The action-to-pairing derivation earns the geometry; the movie language makes that geometry visible in motion.
51. `[warning]` The fluid analogy is useful as a guide to moving patches and rearrangement without compression, but it must not be mistaken for a derivation of symplectic structure.
52. `[warning]` The contour-map, constant-\(H\), 90-degree-turn, generator, and Poisson-bracket stories belong later or only anticipatorily, not as the main derivation of this section.
