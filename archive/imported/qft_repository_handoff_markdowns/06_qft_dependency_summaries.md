# QFT Dependency Summaries

*Repository support note.* This file is not manuscript prose and not meta-workflow material. It is a compact support document for later agents working on the QFT chapter. Its purpose is to summarize only the non-QFT manuscript material that the QFT chapter depends on most heavily, and to make explicit what the QFT chapter imports from those earlier sections. It is therefore selective by design. It does not try to summarize the whole manuscript.

The four dependency zones covered here are:

1. symmetry  
2. relativity  
3. quantum mechanics  
4. action / curvature / gauge

For each, this file records:
- the core concepts QFT assumes,
- the key equations or structural ideas the QFT chapter builds on,
- the terminology the QFT chapter presumes the reader has already seen,
- and the QFT sections that rely on that prior material.

Cross-links below refer to sections in `02_qft_section_draft.md` and to the more explicit structure in `05_mother_outline_updated.md`.

## 1. Symmetry

### Why QFT depends on this section

The QFT chapter does not want to introduce momentum, generators, translation invariance, or representation language from scratch. It assumes that the manuscript’s symmetry material has already taught the reader to think of physical laws in terms of transformations, invariants, and the generators of continuous symmetries. Without that background, several of the most important turns in the QFT chapter would look like arbitrary tricks rather than natural moves.

### Core concepts imported by QFT

#### Transformations and invariance
The symmetry chapter establishes that one asks of a law not merely whether it predicts correctly, but whether it retains form under changes of perspective or transformation. QFT inherits this stance directly. The passage from local fields to their transformation properties, and later the move from global to local symmetry, only makes sense against that background.

#### Generators of continuous symmetry
QFT imports the idea that continuous symmetries are represented infinitesimally by generators. This matters especially for:
- spatial translation symmetry,
- momentum as generator of translation,
- and the later use of symmetry-adapted bases.

Without that, the momentum-space diagonalization of the free field would feel merely computational rather than structurally motivated.

#### Representation thinking
The symmetry chapter prepares the reader to think in terms of objects transforming according to different representations. QFT depends on this later when distinguishing:
- scalar fields,
- spinor fields,
- and gauge fields.

Even when the QFT chapter does not fully unpack representation theory at every point, it assumes the reader has already seen that not all physical quantities transform in the same way.

#### Commutators as structural relations
The symmetry material also helps normalize the idea that commutators are not just algebraic curiosities but encode structural facts about generators and the relations among transformations. QFT relies on that habit of thought when it introduces:
- equal-time canonical commutators,
- operator algebras,
- and later anticommutators for fermionic fields.

### Key equations or structures imported

The exact notation may differ across chapters, but QFT assumes familiarity with the general form of:
- translation operators generated by momentum,
- commutator language for infinitesimal structure,
- and the idea that plane waves are naturally associated with translation eigenstates.

In particular, the QFT draft’s move into momentum space presumes that the reader already accepts, conceptually, the structural relation between translation symmetry and momentum.

### Terminology QFT assumes from symmetry

The QFT chapter assumes the reader has already encountered terms such as:
- symmetry
- generator
- translation invariance
- momentum as generator
- representation
- commutator
- conserved quantity

It may reactivate these terms, but it does not want to define them from zero.

## 2. Relativity

### Why QFT depends on this section

If symmetry is the algebraic precondition for much of QFT, relativity is the geometric and causal precondition. The QFT chapter repeatedly relies on prior relativity material to justify why the theory must be local in spacetime, why the Klein–Gordon equation has the sign structure it does, why the mass shell matters, and why scattering language can be framed in terms of asymptotic relativistic particles.

### Core concepts imported by QFT

#### Minkowski spacetime
The QFT chapter assumes the reader already knows that spacetime is not Euclidean space with time bolted on, but a geometric structure with a mixed-sign interval. This is essential because the chapter later interprets the Klein–Gordon operator as reflecting the same sign structure as the Minkowski interval.

#### Light cones and causal order
The QFT chapter’s insistence on local mediation and microcausality depends directly on the relativity chapter’s treatment of spacelike, timelike, and lightlike separation. Without that, the condition that spacelike-separated observables commute would sound formal rather than physically motivated.

#### Lorentz covariance
The chapter assumes the reader already accepts that physical laws must be compatible with Lorentz transformations. This matters both for:
- why Schrödinger’s equation is structurally the wrong sort of local field equation for relativity,
- and why fields must transform appropriately under spacetime symmetries.

#### Relativistic energy-momentum relation
The QFT chapter inherits the mass-shell structure:
- \(E^2 = p^2 + m^2\) in natural units,
or equivalently with \(c\) restored as needed.

This relation reappears in multiple places:
- in the continuum limit of the lattice model,
- in the dispersion relation for the free field,
- in the interpretation of mode frequency,
- and in the pole structure of propagators.

#### Mass shell as more than an equation
The relativity material also prepares the reader to see the mass shell not just as a formula but as a structural constraint on allowed free-particle states. This becomes important when the QFT chapter later connects propagator poles to asymptotic particle interpretation.

### Key equations or structures imported

The QFT chapter relies heavily on familiarity with:
- the Minkowski interval \(ds^2 = c^2dt^2 - d\mathbf{x}^2\) or equivalent sign convention,
- the relativistic dispersion relation,
- light-cone structure,
- and the d'Alembertian / spacetime derivative logic in covariant form.

The reader does not need all of relativity rederived, but QFT assumes the language is already live.

### Terminology QFT assumes from relativity

The QFT chapter assumes prior familiarity with:
- Lorentz transformation
- Lorentz covariance
- Minkowski spacetime
- light cone
- spacelike / timelike / null
- 4-momentum
- mass shell
- proper time
- relativistic dispersion relation

## 3. Quantum mechanics

### Why QFT depends on this section

The QFT chapter is not trying to overthrow quantum mechanics. It is trying to deepen and relocate it. That means the chapter assumes the earlier QM material already established the central quantum framework: states, observables, amplitudes, operators, Hilbert space, Schrödinger evolution, and unitary structure. QFT then reassigns roles within that framework rather than discarding it.

### Core concepts imported by QFT

#### Hilbert space and state vectors
The QFT chapter assumes the reader already understands that the state of a quantum system is represented in Hilbert space, not by a classical trajectory. This becomes essential when QFT later distinguishes:
- local fields in spacetime,
- from states in Hilbert or Fock space.

Without a prior Hilbert-space chapter, the “spaces of QFT” section would be far harder to land.

#### Observables as operators
QFT assumes the reader already knows what an operator is in the quantum-mechanical sense and why observables are represented by operators. This is the starting point for the operator-in-spacetime route.

#### Schrödinger and Heisenberg pictures
The QFT chapter explicitly uses the transition from the Schrödinger picture to the Heisenberg picture as a conceptual bridge. That move only works if the earlier QM chapter has already made both pictures intelligible and has shown that they are equivalent reorganizations of the same physics.

#### Canonical quantization habits
The QFT chapter imports the reader’s familiarity with canonical quantization:
- promoting classical variables to operators,
- imposing commutators,
- and using the harmonic oscillator as a prototype.

This matters directly for the free-field quantization section.

#### Unitary time evolution
QFT assumes the reader has already seen that quantum dynamics preserve inner products under unitary evolution. The later QFT unitarity section extends this idea rather than introducing it from nothing.

#### Probability and Born interpretation
Even though QFT later speaks in the language of correlators and amplitudes, the interpretive background is still quantum-mechanical: amplitudes eventually feed probabilities. The QFT chapter assumes that the reader already understands this.

### Key equations or structures imported

Again the notation may vary, but QFT assumes the reader already recognizes the significance of:
- the Schrödinger equation,
- the Heisenberg equation of motion,
- canonical commutation relations in simpler systems,
- the quantum harmonic oscillator and ladder operators,
- and unitary time evolution.

The harmonic oscillator is especially important because the field quantization section leans on it heavily. If the oscillator has not already become conceptually familiar, the field’s mode quantization will feel abrupt.

### Terminology QFT assumes from QM

The chapter assumes familiarity with:
- Hilbert space

- observable
- operator
- Schrödinger picture
- Heisenberg picture
- commutator
- unitary evolution
- ladder operator
- vacuum / ground state logic in oscillator context
- expectation value
- amplitude and probability

## 4. Action / curvature / gauge

### Why QFT depends on this section

This dependency is especially important because it supplies the chapter’s preferred explanation of force. The QFT chapter does not want to treat interactions as unexplained insertions. It wants them to arise from local structure, especially from local symmetry and the fields required to preserve it. That makes the action / curvature / gauge material one of the most important pre-QFT dependencies in the manuscript.

### Core concepts imported by QFT

#### Action principle
The QFT chapter assumes the reader already knows what the action is and why classical equations of motion can be derived from it. This becomes crucial again in two places:
- when building the classical field theory from a Lagrangian density,
- and when introducing the path integral, where the action reappears in the amplitude phase.

#### Lagrangian density as local structure
The earlier action / curvature material prepares the reader to think locally: not just in terms of one global energy function, but in terms of densities distributed through spacetime. QFT depends on this when it writes local field theories and later when it introduces interaction terms and gauge couplings.

#### Local symmetry
The gauge bridge material is especially important because the QFT chapter later uses the move from global to local phase symmetry as the reason a gauge field must appear. That logic only lands cleanly if the reader has already seen why local symmetry is structurally different from global symmetry.

#### Covariant derivative intuition
Even if the QFT chapter reintroduces the covariant derivative, it benefits enormously from the reader already having seen the broader idea that ordinary derivatives fail to preserve local symmetry and that a connection field repairs that failure.

#### Curvature / connection mindset
The gauge field as connection is much easier to motivate if earlier material has already normalized the idea that one may need extra geometric structure to compare local data consistently across spacetime or across internal spaces.

#### Force as local structure rather than primitive action-at-a-distance
This is the deepest conceptual dependency here. The earlier chapter material makes it plausible that force should be explained geometrically or covariantly, not merely posited. QFT then cashes that out with actual quantum fields and couplings.

### Key equations or structures imported

QFT depends on prior familiarity with:
- action \(S = \int \mathcal L \, d^4x\) or equivalent local variational form,
- Euler–Lagrange logic for fields,
- the idea of a covariant derivative \(D_\mu\),
- and the distinction between global and local symmetry transformations.

The QFT chapter later uses these without wanting to stop for a full first-principles reintroduction.

### Terminology QFT assumes from action / gauge material

The chapter assumes familiarity with:
- action

- Euler–Lagrange equation
- local symmetry
- gauge symmetry
- covariant derivative
- connection
- field strength / curvature language in broad conceptual terms

## 6. Final repository judgment

The QFT chapter can now be read as the recombination point of four earlier achievements:

- symmetry provided the transformation language,
- relativity provided the causal geometry,
- quantum mechanics provided the amplitude/operator/state-space framework,
- action and gauge material provided the local variational and interaction logic.

A later agent revising the QFT chapter should therefore resist the temptation to treat it as self-contained. It is the convergence point of those earlier sections, and its clearest explanations are the ones that explicitly inherit from them rather than pretending to stand alone.
